Frameless Design (IKAROS Principle)

The mirror has no rigid frame. The design is based on the Japanese solar sail IKAROS (JAXA, 2010).

The mirror rotates around its axis (1-2 rpm). Centrifugal force tensions the foil through cables, turning it into a rigid square. Rotation also acts as a gyroscope, stabilizing orientation in space.

  • Tip masses ‚Äî 4 units at corners, create centrifugal force and stabilize shape
  • Cables ‚Äî connect corners to central hub, transmit tension

Electronics

At the mirror‚Äôs center is one integrated chip the size of a coin (~50 g), delivered from Earth. All functions are combined on a single die:

  • Star tracker ‚Äî determines own orientation
  • Radio beacons ‚Äî positioning relative to other mirrors
  • Onboard processor ‚Äî calculates required pointing angle
  • Photovoltaic power ‚Äî a few cm¬≤ of silicon, provides microwatts for operation

Why from Earth? Manufacturing highly integrated chips requires extremely complex lithographic processes. This is the only component that is impractical to produce in space.

Technology baseline (2025): The radiation-hardened electronics market is $1.6 billion. Modern star trackers weigh tens of grams, solar sensors weigh less than 5 g. Chip-scale satellites (Sprites) weigh only 10 g total. By the 2030s, SoC solutions will combine processor, memory, and sensors in a single chip weighing ~10-20 g.

Attitude Control

The Problem

Billions of mirrors in space. Cannot use conventional thrusters (fuel runs out) or reaction wheels (heavy, fail).

Solution: Light Pressure + Electrochromics

Technology proven by the Japanese solar sail IKAROS (JAXA, 2010).

Principle:

  • The mirror is a solar sail
  • Light exerts pressure on the surface (~60 ŒºPa at Mercury orbit)
  • If one part of the surface reflects better than another ‚Äî a torque is created

Electrochromics:

Along the mirror edges are strips of electrochromic material (TiO‚ÇÇ, titanium oxide). Titanium is mined on Mercury (<1% of surface mass), making production 100% local:

  • Apply current ‚Üí strip darkens ‚Üí absorbs more light ‚Üí pressure on opposite side increases ‚Üí mirror rotates
  • No thrusters, no fuel
  • Energy: microwatts from integrated photovoltaic

Why TiO‚ÇÇ, not WO‚ÇÉ? Tungsten oxide (WO‚ÇÉ) is more efficient, but tungsten was not measured by MESSENGER and its availability is unknown. Titanium is confirmed (~0.5%), and at billion-mirror scale source reliability is critical. TiO‚ÇÇ is less efficient (17% vs 60%), but production is 100% local.

Problem Statement

\[ \eta = \cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) \]

\[ ME = \sqrt{r_M^2 + r_E^2 - 2 \cdot r_M \cdot r_E \cdot \cos(\phi)} \]

Angle Œ∏ at vertex M:

\[ \cos(\theta) = \frac{r_M^2 + ME^2 - r_E^2}{2 \cdot r_M \cdot ME} \]

Disposal

Space Debris Problem

With 10-year service life and 1.1 billion mirrors, ~110 million units fail annually. Without a disposal mechanism, this could create a space debris problem.

Solution: Solar Descent

The mirror is a solar sail. When tilted at an angle to the Sun, it creates a tangential force that slows orbital motion. The orbit spirals down until it burns up in the solar corona.

Failsafe: The mirror design ensures that upon electronics failure, it automatically enters braking mode ‚Äî through asymmetric coating or offset center of mass. Without active electrochromic control, the mirror begins spiraling toward the Sun.

Why not replace at efficiency threshold? Space in the swarm is unlimited. An old mirror at 30% efficiency produces less energy but still works. New mirrors are added, not replaced. Disposal occurs only at complete failure ‚Äî when the mirror becomes uncontrollable.
